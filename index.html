The mechanism, simply explained

Why generic training feels “almost right” — but still doesn’t fully fit.

The problem usually isn’t that you’re doing nothing. It’s that the dog in front of you is a cross between two very different working styles — and those styles can clash inside one dog.

Golden side

Attachment + mouthiness + physical enthusiasm

✓
Grabs with the mouthMore likely to use the mouth during play, excitement, and attention-seeking.
✓
Stays close to youWants to follow, shadow, and remain involved in whatever you are doing.
✓
Gets physically “big” fastJumping, charging, and greeting energy become a bigger issue as the dog grows.
Poodle side

Sensitivity + intensity + independent problem-solving

✓
Feels things stronglyCan become over-aroused, overstimulated, or easily tipped into frustration.
✓
Thinks quicklyLearns cues fast — but fast learning is not the same as emotional regulation.
✓
Pushes back when mishandledHarsh or generic methods can create more chaos instead of calm.

Put those two together, and you get what many owners feel as a “Genetic Tug-of-War.”

One side wants contact, action, and stimulation. The other side is quick, sensitive, and intense. So the same dog can be loving and clever… while also becoming hard to settle, hard to redirect, and weirdly more difficult than the dogs you’ve raised before.
